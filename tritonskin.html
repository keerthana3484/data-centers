01 — THE MECHANISM

How a sealed pod cools itself a kilometre down

No fans, no open loop with the ocean, no moving parts touching seawater directly. Heat leaves the hull the same way it always has underwater — through a temperature difference and a hull wall. Four things happen, in this order, continuously.

01 / INTAKE

Cold water arrives

Ambient seawater near the hull sits colder than the servers inside. That gap is the entire cooling budget — no pumps make it colder, they only move it faster.

02 / EXCHANGE

Heat crosses the hull wall

Server heat conducts through a sealed metal skin into the boundary layer of water hugging it. Fouling on that skin is the enemy here — it insulates exactly where you need conduction.

03 / OUTFLOW

Warm water leaves

Now carrying that heat, the water exits the hull's far side measurably warmer — the plume regulators are built to catch and disperse this before it stresses local marine life.

04 / DISSIPATION

The ocean absorbs it

Deep-ocean thermal mass is enormous — the plume cools exponentially with distance. The open question isn't "does it dissipate," it's "how close is safe for the reef 40m away."

02 — THE HIDDEN LOSS

Biofouling is winning quietly, until you turn on the actuator

Barnacles, algae, and biofilm don't care that step 02 above needs a clean hull. Left alone, fouling resistance climbs toward its asymptote and the pump has to work harder just to hold temperature. This chart is a live physics simulation, not a mockup — press the button and watch the growth curve actually bend.

Ultrasonic actuator: OFF — fouling resistance accumulating toward R_f*.

Fouling resistance follows a Kern–Seaton asymptotic model, the same curve heat-exchanger engineers have used for decades:

R_f(t) = R_f· (1 − e^(−t/τ))

As R_f rises, effective heat transfer U(t) = 1 / (1/U_clean + R_f(t)) falls — meaning the pump quietly draws more power to hold the same server temperature. That's the cost nobody puts on a spec sheet.

Ultrasonic actuation doesn't sterilize the hull to zero. Published antifouling studies show a 40–70% reduction in settlement while active — this simulation runs on that same honest range, not a marketing number.

03 — THE ECOLOGICAL FOOTPRINT

Watching the plume so a regulator doesn't have to ask

Warm outflow spreads and cools by advection-diffusion — fast near the hull, negligible a short distance out. TritonSkin's job is proving exactly where that line sits, continuously, instead of a one-time environmental impact study.

ΔT(r) = ΔT₀ · e^(−r/L)

Temperature above ambient decays exponentially with distance r from the outlet, governed by a decay length L set by current speed and outlet geometry. The rings on the left are that equation, rendered — not decorative circles.
